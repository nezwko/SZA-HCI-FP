Reptiles

Introduction

Reptiles are cold-blooded animals with scaly skin. They breathe air through lungs and most of them lay eggs. Reptiles have been around for millions of years—even when dinosaurs roamed the Earth! Some common reptiles include snakes, lizards, turtles, and crocodiles.

Where Do Reptiles Live?

Reptiles can live in a variety of places!


Lizards and snakes love the hot, sandy deserts.

Crocodiles and turtles often live near rivers and ponds.

Sea turtles live in the ocean but come to land to lay their eggs.

Some reptiles, like geckos, live near people and help eat pesky bugs.

Interesting Features

Here are some things that make reptiles special:

SCALY SKIN

Reptiles have thick, scaly skin that protects them and helps keep their bodies from drying out.

COLD-BLOODED

Reptiles can’t make their own body heat. They get warm by basking in the sun and cool off by hiding in the shade.

EGG-LAYERS

Most reptiles lay eggs with tough shells, but some, like certain snakes, give birth to live young.

SHARP TEETH OR BEAKS

Many reptiles, like crocodiles and snakes, have sharp teeth or beaks for catching food.

LUNGS FOR BREATHING

Like mammals and birds, reptiles use lungs to breathe air.

SLOW METABOLISM

Reptiles don’t need to eat as often as mammals because their bodies use energy more slowly.

Fun Fact!!

The Komodo dragon is the largest lizard in the world—it can grow up to 10 feet long! On the other hand, the smallest reptile, the nano-chameleon, is so tiny it can fit on the tip of your finger.
