Amphibians

Introduction

Amphibians are animals that can live both in water and on land. The word "amphibian" means "double life" because they often start life in water and move to land as they grow. Frogs, toads, salamanders, and newts are examples of amphibians. They are special because their skin helps them breathe!

Where Do Amphibians Live?

Amphibians live in places that are wet or near water because they need moisture to survive!


POND AND LAKE

Frogs and tadpoles live here.

RAINFOREST

Many colorful amphibians, like tree frogs, live in rainforests.

SWAMP AND MARSH

Salamanders love these damp areas.

UNDERGROUND

Some amphibians burrow into the soil to stay cool and moist.

Interesting Features

Here are some things that make amphibians special:

LIFE CYCLE

Amphibians start as eggs in water, hatch into larvae (like tadpoles), and grow into adults with legs and lungs. This process is called metamorphosis.

SMOOTH, MOIST SKIN

Amphibians have soft, wet skin that helps them breathe by soaking up oxygen from the air or water.

COLD-BLOODED

Like reptiles, amphibians can’t keep their bodies warm. They rely on the sun or shade to control their temperature.

JUMPING AND SWIMMING

Frogs and toads are excellent jumpers, while salamanders are great swimmers.

SENSITIVITY

Amphibians need clean water and air to live, so scientists use them to check the health of the environment.

Fun Fact!!

The Goliath frog from Africa is the largest frog in the world—it can grow as big as a cat! Meanwhile, the tiny Paedophryne frog from Papua New Guinea is so small it can sit on a coin.
